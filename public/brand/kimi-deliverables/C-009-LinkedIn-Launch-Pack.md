Not adapted warehouses with projectors rented for six weeks. Not pop-ups in shopping centres. A purpose-built space where the architecture, the technology, and the artistic vision are designed together.

We're building that now.

## POST 2: CREATIVE DIRECTOR INTRODUCTION — Kev Freeney / IDIRNET

**Topic:** Technical vision and creative practice  
**Angle:** 15+ years in immersive, the infrastructure vision, technical-creative hybrid  
**Best Time:** Thursday, 12:00 PM (lunch break, professional development mindset)

---

**Opening Hook:**
I've been building immersive spaces for fifteen years. Lightheart is the first time I've had a blank canvas.

---

**Body:**
Not metaphorically blank. Actually blank.

Every immersive project I've worked on previously was adaptation — projectors rigged to existing architecture, speakers shoehorned into corners, networks patched over inherited infrastructure. Brilliant work happened, but always within constraints not of our making.

Lightheart is different.

I'm Creative Director of IDIRNET, the technical team who designed Lightheart's infrastructure. We specified every element: 37 Barco projectors at precise angles, 87 L-Acoustics speakers in L-ISA configuration, 44 Luxonis depth cameras, five Pixera servers for real-time rendering. Not because big numbers impress, but because specific infrastructure enables specific art.

The technical is creative.

When an artist knows exactly which projectors cover which surfaces, they design for that canvas. When they understand L-ISA can place 96 audio objects in three-dimensional space, they compose for that possibility. When they realise sensors detect presence in real-time, they create responsive environments, not video loops.

We don't rent equipment to artists. We collaborate technically. IDIRNET's role is to make the technology disappear — so only the art remains.

If you're an artist working with real-time systems, spatial audio, or interactive environments, I'd love to show you what we've built. The technical specification is 70 pages, but the conversation is simpler: what do you want to make that you couldn't make before?

What would you create if the infrastructure was designed specifically for your work?

## POST 3: THE SPACE — Architecture and Venue Announcement

**Topic:** Purpose-built announcement with credibility numbers  
**Angle:** Investment scale, architectural specificity, quarterly programming model  
**Best Time:** Monday, 8:30 AM (week start, institutional audiences active)

---

**Opening Hook:**
Purpose-built means something specific. It means EUR 4-4.5 million invested before a single visitor walks through the door.

---

**Body:**
Lightheart is not a converted warehouse.

The ceiling height — 4.2 metres — was calculated for optimal projection angles. The floor load-bearing capacity accounts for sensor arrays and equipment. The walls are treated surfaces for projection mapping, not painted plaster. The HVAC system handles 37 projectors' heat output. The network architecture supports uncompressed 4K video across 100G backbone.

This is 280 square metres designed, funded, and constructed specifically for immersive art.

The programming model is equally deliberate. Quarterly exhibitions — four per year, 12 weeks each. Not because we lack content, but because we believe in depth over distraction. Each exhibition finds its audience, evolves through its run, and is properly documented before the next begins.

The economics are challenging. Cultural infrastructure always is. But the alternative — another generation of Irish artists leaving to create work abroad because the spaces don't exist here — is more expensive.

We're currently in final construction phase. September 2026, we open with our first exhibition. The technical infrastructure is being installed through June and July. Artist commissioning begins immediately.

For funders, tourism bodies, and institutional partners: this is permanent infrastructure. A cultural asset for Dublin that will operate for decades. If you're interested in partnership opportunities, exhibition sponsorship, or long-term collaboration, I'd welcome a conversation.

What does purpose-built cultural infrastructure enable that adapted spaces cannot?

## POST 4: OPEN CALL SIGNAL — Commissioning Philosophy

**Topic:** Signalling that Lightheart commissions work  
**Angle:** Artist brief philosophy, how we work with artists, link to /artists  
**Best Time:** Wednesday, 2:00 PM (mid-week, creative professionals active)

---

**Opening Hook:**
We're not renting our space to artists. We're commissioning work specifically for it.

---

**Body:**
Lightheart's exhibitions are commissioned, not curated from existing work. We collaborate with artists from concept through installation to create experiences that engage our specific technical infrastructure.

What does that collaboration look like?

We start with the technical specification — 37 projectors at defined angles, L-ISA spatial audio, real-time sensor systems — not as constraints, but as creative parameters. Artists who understand these affordances can make work that simply couldn't exist elsewhere.

IDIRNET's technical team works alongside artists throughout development. Real-time prototyping. Technical consultation. System integration. The infrastructure becomes a collaborator, not just a venue.

Our curatorial philosophy centres on four principles: embodied participation (you're inside the work, not in front of it), purpose-built specificity (work designed for this exact space), technogenetic integration (technology as co-author), and negentropic practice (duration and depth over fragmentation).

If this approach resonates with your practice, we've published a detailed Artist Brief at lightheart.ie/artists. It includes our technical specification, commissioning process, and selection criteria.

We're currently reviewing proposals for 2027 exhibitions. Our 2026 programming is confirmed, but we're beginning conversations now for next year's quarterly shows.

What would you create if you had infrastructure designed specifically for immersive work?
