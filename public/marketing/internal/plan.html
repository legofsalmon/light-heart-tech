2. Curatorial Philosophy & Brand DNA

Four founding principles that govern all communications and programming:

Principle 1: Independence

Lightheart retains full editorial and curatorial independence. External funding is acceptable but never at the cost of programmatic freedom. The gallery will never become dependent on funding streams that compromise this.

Comms implication: Messaging must signal strength of conviction without being combative. Position as self-funded, self-directed, beholden to no agenda.

Principle 2: Future Optimism

The gallery embraces AI, robotics, spatial audio, 3D, sensor technology — not to warn about the future but to show what's possible. Counter the prevailing doom narrative in contemporary art.

Comms implication: Tone should be energising and forward-looking. Avoid catastrophism. Show technology as empowering. This is a differentiator from most digital art spaces.

Principle 3: Awe

Collective wonder over individual spectacle. Communal experiences where audiences feel profound awe together. Constantly push boundaries of what's technically possible.

Comms implication: Visual content must convey scale and wonder. BTS (behind-the-scenes) content showing the mechanism builds credibility. The phrase "sell understanding, not just magic" should guide content strategy.

Principle 4: Pluralism

Everyone's story can be told. Ideas should be challenged through art, not censored. Resist the culture of cancellation. Focus on shared humanity rather than identity categories.

Comms implication: Programming announcements should emphasise breadth of perspective. Avoid culture-war framing but don't shy from provocative work.

3. Process & Method: The Unified Toolkit

Lightheart uses six methodological tools in tandem. Five are drawn from marketing strategy. One — the Triple Stack Model — comes from Kev Freeney's practice-based research into embodied perception and immersive art. None of these tools operates alone. They are lenses applied together across every phase, every deliverable, every decision.

The Six Tools at a Glance

ToolSourceWhat It Does for UsWhen We Reach for It
Triple Stack Model (TSM) Kev Freeney, MA Thesis (NCAD/UL 2025) Three-layer audit: infrastructure → embodied perception → relational space. Ensures the experience is coherent from the ground up. Exhibition design, space planning, visitor journey, staff training, opening checklists
Alchemy Rory Sutherland Psycho-logic over logic. Reframe how people perceive value. Small interventions, outsized effects. Pricing perception, queue design, naming, counter-intuitive positioning, scarcity framing
Purple Cow / Sneezers Seth Godin Be remarkable or be invisible. Identify and activate the people who spread the word. Launch mechanics, Founding Circle, referral loops, content that earns attention
6 Principles of Persuasion Robert Cialdini Reciprocity, commitment, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity. The mechanics of trust and action. CTA design, testimonials, early-bird offers, partnership leverage, newsletter copy
Story-Driven / Fortune Cookie Bernadette Jiwa Start with how the customer feels, not what you sell. Difference + resonance = brand. Brand narrative, tone of voice, website copy, press angles, emotional sequencing
RACE Model Helen McNulty / Smart Insights Reach → Act → Convert → Engage. Structured funnel for digital marketing ops. Channel planning, KPI setting, campaign structure, analytics dashboards, attribution

Every major deliverable in this plan can be stress-tested against all six. If a decision only satisfies one tool, it's probably incomplete.

The Triple Stack Model — In Detail

The TSM provides a three-layer audit for any experience, exhibition, or event. It asks: Are the conditions right? Does the body know? Is the transition handled?

Global Stack — Infrastructure & Systems

What has to be true before anyone walks in. The room's readiness.

PlaneOperational Check
GroundElectrical, HVAC, safety — the felt capacity of the room
RuntimeMedia server, clock sync, playback — the room's beat
CirculationVisitor flow, entry sequence, dwell timing, exit legibility
ChannelsConsent protocol, photography policy, GDPR, recording disclosure
FramesSightlines, luminance, caption readability, response timing
RolesStaff positions, visitor roles, steward training
HorizonsQuarterly review cycle, metric definitions, programme direction

Internal Stack — Embodied Perception

What the visitor feels in their body. The somatic pacing of the experience.

PlaneOperational Check
RootFloor surface, seating, temperature — physical grounding
SacralExhibition pacing, emotional arc, transition timing
Solar PlexusVisitor choice architecture, opt-in/opt-out, intensity zones
HeartCo-regulation, group pacing, shared perception moments
ThroatStaff language guide, signage tone, visitor communication
Third EyeAttention design, gaze direction, flicker safety
CrownExit experience, reflection space, post-visit integration

External Stack — Transition & Relational Space

How visitors move from the outside world into the immersive space and back out.

PlaneOperational Check
SpaceSightline testing, route mapping, spatial verification
Portal (Threshold)Entry count-in, consent line, exit cue visibility
GestureMovement pacing, accessible alternatives, abort protocol
MirrorFeedback systems, echo design, visitor state indicators
NarrativeVisitor journey arc, change log, offboarding sequence
AtmosphereLuminance/warmth/air ranges, SPL caps, quiet pockets
Feedback LoopLearning cycles, data collection, posted change process

Concrescence — When the Stacks Converge

The moment when all three stacks align into temporary unity — infrastructure, perception, and participation cohere. Opening night is the first concrescence. Each exhibition opening thereafter is another. The quarterly rhythm creates a cycle the city can organise around.

T.E.M.P.O. — The Communication Method

A practical tool for introducing any unfamiliar concept — to staff, press, public, or stakeholders. Use it when explaining "immersive art," "spatial audio," "quarterly programming," or anything visitors may not yet understand.

StepActionExample: "Immersive Art"
TermDefine in plain language"Art that surrounds you — projected on every surface, with sound that moves through the room."
EmergenceWhere does this come from?"Started with teamLab in Japan, Meow Wolf in the US. Now a global movement."
MaterialisationHow does it appear at Lightheart?"14 projectors, 50 speakers, floors included. You walk through it."
PositioningHow does it relate to what people know?"Not a museum. Not a theme park. The gallery where you stand inside the art."
OperationHow does it function in our system?"New exhibition every quarter. Each one a completely new world."

T.E.M.P.O. works alongside Jiwa's story-driven method (lead with feeling) and Sutherland's reframing (find the psycho-logical angle). It's how we translate technical capability into human language.

4E Cognition — Why the Experience Works

The underlying theory of perception that informs TSM and, by extension, every experience design decision at Lightheart:

  • Embodied: Exhibitions engage the body, not just the eye — this is why the floor is projected too
  • Embedded: The gallery's physical environment actively patterns attention and movement
  • Extended: Technology (projectors, spatial audio) participates in perception — it's not a screen you watch, it's an extension of how you sense the room
  • Enactive: The visitor's experience is enacted through their movement, not passively received — this is what separates Lightheart from cinema

In marketing terms: 4E is why "you have to be there" works as a message. You can't replicate this on a screen. That's the product's built-in purple cow (Godin) and its natural scarcity (Cialdini).

4. Strategic Positioning

Core Strategic Mantra

Build credibility first.
Build clarity second.
Build noise last.

This sequence, established in the DCC strategy session, governs the entire marketing rollout.

Layer 1 — The Core Product (Homepage level)

  • A world-class immersive gallery in Dublin
  • Quarterly changing exhibitions
  • High quality audiovisual work
  • Ticketed cultural destination
  • Clear visitor promise

Layer 2 — The Ambition (About page level)

  • Community infrastructure for digital art
  • Digital art advocacy & policy positioning
  • Education alignment (NCAD, Trinity, schools)
  • R&D possibilities
  • Future pipeline & artist development

Rule: If the ambition narrative overtakes the core offer, you lose clarity. Layer 2 enriches Layer 1 but never replaces it.

Competitive Landscape

  • 180 Strand, London
  • Atelier des Lumieres, Paris
  • Belfast immersive warehouse project (Visual Spectrum)

Irish Landscape:

  • No comparable high-standard immersive gallery in Dublin
  • Existing local examples: gimmicky, child-focused, rental-based
  • Lightheart positioned as raising the national standard

The Real Competition

Lightheart is competing for leisure time and cultural attention against:

  • Netflix / streaming
  • Cinema
  • Phone dopamine loops
  • The RDS Van Gogh touring model
  • Tourist attractions
  • Traditional cultural institutions

Key Differentiator: Quarterly Changeover

Illusion galleries don't change. Van Gogh touring shows rarely change. Tourist attractions are static. Quarterly programming gives Lightheart:

  • Freshness & repeat visits
  • Membership potential
  • Media hooks every quarter
  • An ongoing story to tell
  • Cultural rhythm

Rory Sutherland — Alchemy (2019)

"Engineering doesn't allow for magic. Psychology does."

Full analysis: book-analyses/01-alchemy-sutherland.md

Core thesis: Human beings are psycho-logical, not logical. The most valuable marketing solutions live in the "doesn't make sense but works" quadrant — things competitors will never copy because they seem too irrational to adopt.

Key frameworks:

  • Psycho-Logic — Build for how people feel, not what they rationally evaluate
  • The Sense vs. Works Matrix — The top-right cell (works but doesn't make sense) is where alchemy lives
  • Costly Signaling — For a signal to be trusted, it must be costly to fake (premium price, limited access, deliberate inefficiency)
  • Satisficing — Humans seek "good enough across many dimensions," not optimised on one. Design the full arc of the visit, not one peak metric
  • Reframing — Change the reference point and you change the perceived value

10 Lightheart applications (summary):

  1. Signal exclusivity through quarterly curation, not volume — never discount
  2. Reframe the competition — position against a European trip, not Dublin attractions
  3. Make the technology invisible but felt — specs for press, wonder for visitors
  4. Test one counterintuitive tactic per quarter (silent sessions, permanent waitlist, "not for everyone" campaign)
  5. Build reciprocity before the sale — preview events, documentaries, subscriber-first access
  6. Make every detail a signal — email, ticket, lobby scent, wayfinding
  7. Embrace strategic weirdness — a pressed flower, an unexplained ritual
  8. Use the satisficing gap — design for 90 satisfying minutes, not 60 optimised ones
  9. Let journalists do the explaining — brief critics privately, let their frames become the public's frames
  10. Create logic-proof word of mouth — moments impossible to describe are moments that generate referral

Seth Godin — Purple Cow (2003)

"In a crowded marketplace, fitting in is failing. Not standing out is the same as being invisible."

Full analysis: book-analyses/02-purple-cow-godin.md

Core thesis: The TV-Industrial Complex is dead. In a saturated market, being competent is the fastest route to invisibility. Remarkability must be designed into the product itself, not bolted on through advertising.

Key frameworks:

  • The Idea Diffusion Curve — Target the 15-20% of innovators and early adopters ("sneezers"), not the mass market. If sneezers adopt, the mass follows. Design for the mass, and sneezers ignore you
  • The Otaku Principle — Genuine obsession produces remarkable things. Don't ask "what does the market want?" — ask "what are we truly obsessed with?"
  • The Magic Cycle — (1) Design remarkable experience → (2) Market to smallest viable audience of sneezers → (3) Sneezers spread through peer networks → (4) Reinvest; never rest on previous cow
  • Three Eras of Marketing — Before Advertising (be good), During Advertising (out-spend), After Advertising (be remarkable). We are firmly in era three

10 Lightheart applications (summary):

  1. Build remarkability into the experience itself — design 5-7 moments per exhibition visitors cannot help describing
  2. Target sneezers — Dublin's arts journalists, design influencers, tech leaders, cultural connectors. Map them before launch
  3. Embrace controversy — not everyone will understand the price or the proposition. Sharpen, don't soften
  4. Focus on the most valuable niche — culturally-engaged 30-55 with international travel experience and disposable income
  5. Make the tech visible enough to be shareable — give visitors vocabulary ("spatial audio — the sound actually moves around you")
  6. Build a permission asset — curated email list that compounds quarterly
  7. Never rest on the first cow — Q2 2027 exhibition should already be in development at September 2026 launch
  8. Cross-industry innovation — apply hospitality, gaming, fashion cadence thinking to the art world
  9. Design for the "difficult to describe" moment — when visitors say "you just have to go," the purple cow is working
  10. Make the quarterly cycle itself remarkable — announce with restraint, build through silence, train the audience to anticipate

Self-assessment: Lightheart scores strongly across all 7 dimensions of remarkability (technology, content, venue, ticket model, community, cadence, word-of-mouth). The only risk is execution conservatism — softening the proposition to fill capacity.

Robert Cialdini — Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion (1984/2021)

Full analysis: book-analyses/03-persuasion-cialdini.md

Core thesis: Humans have evolved cognitive shortcuts ("click, whirr") triggered by specific environmental cues. Six universal principles drive all compliance decisions. Understanding them allows ethical, principled design of communications and experiences that earn genuine trust.

The 6 Principles — Lightheart tactical summary:

PrincipleMechanismLightheart Primary Tactic
ReciprocationA gift creates a debtFree preview events for 100 cultural influencers; behind-the-scenes newsletter; personal follow-up notes from artistic director
Commitment & ConsistencySmall yeses lead to big yesesProgressive ladder: Follow → Subscribe → Preview → Ticket → Membership. Booking friction (selecting session, confirming) deepens commitment
Social ProofUncertainty → look to othersDisplay sold-out sessions; facilitate visitor photography; collect specific testimonials ("my son asked when we could go back"); waitlist as demand signal
AuthorityDeference to expertise signalsCurator credentials visible; institutional partnerships (NCAD, Trinity); production quality as authority signal; critical press coverage as authority transfer
LikingWe say yes to people we likeHost/ambassador programme (10-15 genuine Dublin cultural figures); warm staff; artist visibility through talks and Q&As; write like a person, not a brand
ScarcityRare = valuableQuarterly exhibitions with hard end dates; session capacity limits; founding membership limited to 200; no-discount policy ever

3-Phase tactical playbook:

  • Pre-Launch: Reciprocation (free previews) + Authority (curator profiles, partnerships) + Scarcity (founding member cap) + Commitment (newsletter ladder)
  • Launch & Year 1: Social Proof (attendance data, waitlists) + Scarcity (countdown to exhibition close) + Authority (press coverage)
  • Community & Retention: Commitment (membership identity) + Liking (ambassador programme) + Social Proof (testimonial collection)

Key insight for Lightheart: All six principles align naturally with the authentic proposition. The scarcity is real (quarterly exhibitions end), the authority is earned (if curation is excellent), the social proof is organic (if the experience is remarkable). The framework doesn't manufacture persuasion — it ensures the genuine substance is visible.

Bernadette Jiwa — 6-Book Synthesis

Full analysis: book-analyses/04-bernadette-jiwa-synthesis.md

Core thesis across all works: Marketing is not about being seen — it is about mattering. The organisations that endure do not start with what they make; they start with the change they exist to create in people's lives. Products are cookies. Meaning is the fortune. Lead with the fortune.

2. Story-Driven vs Competition-Driven:

Competition-Driven (Avoid)Story-Driven (Do This)
"Better than illusion galleries""A new category of cultural experience"
"Dublin's answer to TeamLab""The space Dublin has been waiting for"
"More projectors than anyone else""Technology in service of collective wonder"

3. The Difference Model: Truth → People → Idea → Launch (not Idea → Market)

  • Truth: People are chronically starved of genuine wonder. Digital life delivers novelty but not awe.
  • People: The designer who went to TeamLab. The art teacher seeking embodied experiences. The conference organiser wanting a unique event.
  • Idea: A quarterly-changing immersive gallery that makes wonder accessible and repeatable.

4. Lightheart's Story of Change:

  • Before: Art in Ireland is something you look at across a respectful distance. Digital art is misunderstood, underfunded, and patronised. Nowhere in Dublin for world-class immersive work.
  • Because Lightheart exists: Dublin has a dedicated space where art moves through your body. Artists have infrastructure. Audiences have awe.
  • What this enables: Wonder is not reserved for other cities. Artists build careers at home. Ireland's cultural capacity grows. The quarterly rhythm creates a cultural heartbeat the city organises around.

10 questions Jiwa would ask the team:

  1. What is the truth that Lightheart was built to answer?
  2. What business are you actually in? (Not "immersive gallery" — that's the cookie.)
  3. Who is your single most specific "someone"?
  4. What is your y-axis? (What non-revenue metric tells you you've succeeded?)
  5. What story does a visitor get to tell about themselves?
  6. What is your backstory, and does it reveal your purpose?
  7. What emotional signal will visitors feel before entering the building?
  8. What specific moments are you engineering inside each exhibition?
  9. Is Lightheart competition-driven or story-driven?
  10. What will be different about Dublin because Lightheart existed?
